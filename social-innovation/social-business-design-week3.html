1. From Scaling Enterprises to Scaling Impact

Week 2 covered how to scale the social enterprise itself‚Äîthe 5Rs model, speed factors, and the cautionary tale of OLPC. Week 3 shifts focus from scaling the organisation to scaling IMPACT.

The key question: how do we know our SE is actually creating social value at scale?

This lecture covers three interconnected themes:

  • How social impact is defined (and why definitions matter)
  • How to measure impact effectively
  • The Theory of Change framework as a core tool

2. Defining Social Impact

Social impact has no single agreed definition‚Äîwhich creates challenges for both research and practice.

Multiple definitions from the literature:

Broad view

Changes to people's way of life, culture, community, political systems, environment, health, well-being, rights, fears, and aspirations.

Centre for Social Impact

"The net effect of an activity on a community and well-being of individuals and families."

This emphasises a long-term, systemic view‚Äîlooking at community-level effects, not just individual beneficiaries.

Roche (1999)

"Significant or lasting changes in people's lives brought about by a given action."

Key insight: proportionality matters‚Äîthe change must be meaningful relative to someone's life circumstances.

Clifford (2014) ‚Äî Four Key Elements

#ElementSource
1Value created as consequence of someone's activityEmerson et al. (2000)
2Value experienced by beneficiaries AND all others affectedKolodinsky et al. (2006)
3Includes both positive and negative effectsWainwright (2002)
4Judged against a benchmark/counterfactualClifford (2014)
Key Challenge

"The lack of consensus on the definition of social impact and the best way to measure it hampers both academic debate and practical usage of social impact methods." ‚Äî Maas and Liket (2011)

3. Why Measure Impact?

Impact measurement serves two fundamental purposes:

üîç PROVE

Establish legitimacy and accountability

Demonstrate to stakeholders that real value is being created. Build credibility with funders, governments, and the public.

Nicholls (2009)

üìà IMPROVE

Basis for learning and improving efficiency

Understand what's working and what isn't. Enable data-driven decisions and continuous improvement.

Dees (2007); Paton (2003)

Additional Benefits

  • Performance assessment & adaptation ‚Äî identify growth opportunities, adjust when results don't meet expectations
  • Understanding real change ‚Äî see what's actually happening in stakeholders' lives, not just what you hope is happening
  • Identifying unintended consequences ‚Äî both positive (amplify them) and negative (mitigate them)
  • Building outcomes-focused culture ‚Äî shift from measuring activities/procedures to measuring actual change
  • Stakeholder engagement ‚Äî motivates employees, builds trust with beneficiaries and partners

4. Starting Point: Founder Intentionality

Impact measurement must begin with what the founder intended to achieve. You can only assess performance against the founder's intended priorities and value creation.

Founders express intentionality through:

Purpose

Why the organisation exists and whose interests it serves.

Edmans (2021)

BHAGs ‚Äî Big Hairy Audacious Goals

Vision statements that stretch the organisation beyond what seems possible.

Characteristics:

  • Linked to "strategy as stretch" (Hamel)
  • The "Hubris Factor" ‚Äî visionary companies have overbearing confidence
  • Clear finish line ‚Äî should be able to determine when achieved
  • 50-70% probability of success ‚Äî not a sure bet

Collins & Porras

Mission Statements

Formula: "We [verb] to [target population] to [outcome that can be measured]"

Example: "Providing insect squatters to BOP customers to minimise disease contraction"

Visionary Founders

Muhammad Yunus (Grameen Bank) and Bill Drayton (Ashoka) exemplify founders whose clear intentionality shaped global movements in social enterprise and social entrepreneurship.

5. Critical Performance Variables

From intentionality, derive the CPVs (Critical Performance Variables) ‚Äî the most important things to measure that indicate whether you're achieving your mission.

Example: Lonely-students.com

BHAG

"Eradicate all student loneliness that challenges mental health in UK universities"

Mission

"Provide and facilitate virtual and physical opportunities to meet and build community for all lonely students based on their interests in a safe environment"

Critical Performance Variables
  • # students reached
  • # connections made
  • Loneliness reduction metrics
  • Mental health improvement indicators

6. The Complications ‚Äî Beyond Intentionality

Impact measurement is NOT just about founder intentionality. Four major complications make it significantly harder:

A. Stakeholder Pressures

Multiple stakeholders have different motives and metric preferences.

Key insight: Measures are NOT neutral ‚Äî they reflect particular points of view.

  • Funders are the primary drivers of what gets measured (Moxham, 2010; Nicholls, 2010)
  • "Accountability upwards" ‚Äî SEs are held accountable to resource providers, not their own intentions (Ebrahim, 2003a)
  • Trade-off: funder requirements vs social mission
  • Multiple funders with different goals = complexity
  • Geographic/cultural distance makes it harder for funders to understand local context

B. Capacity Constraints

Impact assessment requires intensive data collection and sophisticated analysis ‚Äî often beyond the reach of financially-constrained SEs.

  • SEs operate in dynamic environments ‚Äî constantly testing and iterating (William Easterly's "searchers")
  • Time/resource trade-off: serving beneficiaries vs measuring impact
  • Leadership attention split between operations and measurement

C. Attribution Challenges

Hard to calculate impact precisely due to exogenous factors.

  • SEs operate in an ecosystem with other organisations ‚Äî hard to attribute impact to a specific actor
  • Requires a counterfactual ‚Äî what would have happened WITHOUT the intervention?
  • Gold standard: Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) (Duflo et al., 2008)
  • But RCTs are expensive, require specialized skills, large participation, and long time to plan/execute

D. Standardisation Pressures

External pressures from market actors, industry bodies, and professional networks push toward standardised metrics.

Key frameworks: IRIS, GIIRS, B Lab Certification, UN SDGs

Standardisation helps comparability but can place additional requirements on SEs.

Three responses to standardisation:

ResponseDescription
ResistanceIgnore or contest demands (defensive, has opportunity cost)
ConformityAdopt as "best practice", calculated compliance, manage risks
StrategicLeverage for competitive advantage, attract sophisticated investors
Warning

Danger of pseudo-impact which is primarily aimed at satisfying stakeholder demands rather than substantively solving fundamental social problems.

7. Theory of Change ‚Äî The Core Framework

A Theory of Change (ToC) is an evidence-based summary (diagram or statement) of how you believe you can make positive change happen.

  • Shows the path from current state (problem) to future state (improvement)
  • Converts a business idea into "provable" social impact
  • Investors, boards, and foundations will require one

The Impact Logic Chain (Ebrahim & Rangan, 2010)

Inputs ‚Üí Activities ‚Üí Outputs ‚Üí Outcomes ‚Üí Impact

Definitions

ElementDefinitionExample
Outputs Numerical counts of actions, products delivered, people served # apps downloaded, # meals served
Outcomes Meaningful changes for population served ‚Äî knowledge, skills, attitudes, behaviour, condition Reduction in weight, increase in confidence, % keeping jobs
Impact Long-term results directly due to outcomes, broader societal effects Reduction in lifestyle diseases, better quality of life
Key Insight

Outputs are easy (just count), but don't confuse them with actual change. Outcomes show change BECAUSE of your program. Impact is long-term and attributed to you.

8. ToC Assumptions

Two types of assumptions underpin every Theory of Change:

1. Assumptions about the Solution

Will the proposed activities work as intended?

Does the intervention actually lead to the expected outputs and outcomes?

2. Assumptions about Context/Beneficiary

What pre-existing conditions are necessary?

What do we assume about the beneficiaries' circumstances, behaviours, or environment?

Example: Financial App for BOP

Designing a financial app for women vs men at the Base of the Pyramid requires different assumptions:

Men respond to...Women prefer...
DataCommunity support
ControlSecurity
CompetitionEducation
RiskLong-term goals
GrowthSocially responsible investing

Tips for Identifying Assumptions

  • Don't identify too many assumptions
  • Focus on assumptions that are:
    • Most critical to success
    • Highest risk
    • Most unstable
    • Least known
    • Have implications for long-term sustainability

9. ToC Template & Example

Template Structure

Intentionality: [statement]

Problem/Need: [2-3 key elements]

Logic Chain:
Activities ‚Üí Outputs ‚Üí Outcomes ‚Üí Impact

Assumptions: [key assumptions]

Example: Weight-Loss Travel App

Target

Professional regular travellers (consultants) involved in food tourism

Problem/Need
  • Healthy at home / unhealthy abroad
  • Don't work out when travelling
  • Business trips disrupt healthy routines
Solution

App with local healthy food/gym finder, challenges, premium dietician concierge

Key Assumption

Solution must be time neutral ‚Äî busy professionals won't adopt solutions that require significant additional time investment

10. Analysing the Theory of Change (Mayne's Framework)

Assess your ToC along five dimensions ‚Äî look for both positive AND negative externalities:

DimensionWhat to Assess
Reach Are intended target groups being reached? Any unexpected groups affected (positively or negatively)?
Capacity Does the org/stakeholders have capacity to execute? Unexpected gaps or strengths? Ripple effects on local capacity?
Behavioral Change Unintended behaviour changes? Spillover effects on norms or dependencies?
Direct Benefits Unexpected positive/negative benefits? Impact on broader ecosystem?
Wellbeing Change Long-term positive/negative consequences? Influence on indirect stakeholders?

11. SSIR Article ‚Äî Scaling Social Impact

Summary of key points from the Stanford Social Innovation Review article on scaling social impact.

The Challenge

Bill Strickland Example

MacArthur "genius" grant recipient, envisioned 100 "franchises" of his Manchester Craftsmen's Guild model in 30 years. By 2003, only a few attempts had been made ‚Äî and most had failed.

Lisbeth Schorr

"We have learned to create small exceptions that change hundreds of lives. But we have not learned how to make the exceptions the rule to change millions."

Defining an Innovation for Scale

Innovations can take three forms:

FormDefinitionExample
Organizational Model Overarching structure for mobilizing people and resources Community Foundations (Frederick Goff, 1914)
Program Integrated set of actions serving a specific purpose Boot Camp for New Dads ‚Äî detailed package, 180+ sites
Principles General guidelines and values KIPP's Five Pillars ‚Äî flexible application, 32 schools

Key Questions Before Scaling

  • What makes the approach distinctive?
  • What is essential to success?
  • What internal/external factors play supporting roles?
  • What could change without jeopardizing impact?
  • How transferable is it?
Bradach's "Minimum Critical Specification"

Define the fewest elements possible to produce the desired impact. Don't over-specify ‚Äî identify only what's essential.

Mechanisms for Spreading Impact (Continuum)

MechanismDescriptionControlResources
Dissemination Provide information and technical assistance Low Low
Affiliation Formal network agreements (loose coalition to franchise-like) Medium Medium
Branching Create local sites through one large org (company-owned stores) High High

12. Key Takeaways

  • Impact measurement serves both PROVE and IMPROVE purposes
  • Start with founder intentionality but navigate stakeholder, capacity, attribution, and standardisation challenges
  • Theory of Change is the core tool ‚Äî logic chain from inputs to impact
  • Assumptions (solution + context) underpin every ToC
  • Analyse ToC for positive AND negative externalities across reach, capacity, behaviour, benefits, wellbeing
  • When scaling impact, consider how to define the innovation (org/program/principles) and mechanism (dissemination/affiliation/branching)
  • Minimum critical specification: define only what's necessary
Remember

Outputs ‚â† Outcomes ‚â† Impact. Counting activities is easy. Proving lasting change is hard ‚Äî but that's what separates social enterprises that scale impact from those that just scale operations.
