1. Recap ‚Äî Why Scaling Matters

Social enterprises exist to address institutional voids and market failures, but they face the unique hybridity challenges discussed in Week 1.

The course now shifts from what social enterprises are to how they scale impact sustainably‚Äîwithout eroding mission or resilience.

Strategy Definition

To overcome these challenges, social enterprises must employ strategies ‚Äî purposeful efforts to guide, steer, manage and control individual actors to achieve greater impact in a financially sustainable way.

2. The Scaling Inhibitors Framework

Key principle: separate signal from noise ‚Äî differentiate between tame problems, wicked problems, and table-stakes issues.

The categories of inhibitors

Scaling friction appears across market demand, finance, ecosystem constraints, impact measurement, product fit, leadership cognition, competitive strategy, and internal operations.

External (Beyond Your Control)

Demand side / Affordability (Customer): limited willingness or ability to pay.

Attracting Finance (Financial): capital misalignment, scarcity, donor dependence.

Ecosystem challenges (External/Macro): macro-economy, infrastructure gaps, formal institutions (judiciary), informal institutions (norms, culture).

Impact Measurement (Social Good): weak or costly evidence systems.

Internal (Within Your Control)

Value Proposition fit (Product/Service): weak problem-solution-market match.

Managerial (Cognition & Decision Making): biases, scarcity mindset, path dependence, overspecialising, unprepared skills.

Competitiveness (Strategic): market size, USP clarity, competitive threats.

Internal (Operational): staffing bottlenecks, process gaps, supply chain friction, customer experience breakdowns.

3. Four Probes for Scaling Assessment

1. Internal Alignment

What elements of the internal running collectively inhibit scaling?

2. Effective Scaling Planning & Execution

Is the leadership/team capable of effecting scaling?

3. Competitive Sustainability

Is the change directionally sound to make us competitive long-term?

4. Positive Impact

Will the organization become less engaged with impact through scaling?

4. The 5Rs Model ‚Äî Should You Scale?

RDefinitionWhat it means in practice
Readiness Internal preparedness ‚Äî right structures, systems, leadership. Operational/organizational stability is crucial before scaling.
Receptivity External environment's openness ‚Äî cultural, political, institutional factors. Assess if the new market/region can absorb the model.

Korten (1980) Progression Model

CBO cycle: Effectiveness first ‚Üí Efficiency ‚Üí Then Expansion. Get good at impact, then get efficient with resources, then scale.

Connection to OLI Framework (Dunning)

Scaling internationally requires Ownership advantages, Location fit, and Internalization capability‚Äîmirroring the 5Rs through an international lens.

5. Speed of Scaling Up (Kubzansky 2013)

1. Supply Push vs Demand Pull

Demand pull = easier and faster diffusion.

2. Maturity of Innovation

Less mature innovations are costlier and scale slower.

3. Marketing Channels

Existing channels = faster; new channels = slower.

4. Urban vs Rural Context

Urban density drives faster diffusion and adoption.

6. üìñ OLPC Case Study ‚Äî Vision vs. Reality

The Vision

2005: Nicholas Negroponte (MIT professor) launches One Laptop Per Child (OLPC).

Model: zero-dividend social enterprise.

Product: XO laptops ‚Äî rugged, simple, educational software + programming tools, designed for resource-scarce environments.

Tagline: ‚Äúa laptop for every child.‚Äù

Core belief: access to technology ‚Üí autonomous learning ‚Üí better future.

Born global: Peru, Uruguay, Rwanda signed intents to purchase millions.

Revenue model: donor-funded development costs + laptop sales revenue.

2005 OLPC launched by Negroponte; bold $100 laptop promise.
2007 XO prototype rollout; early government purchase commitments.
2009 Millions distributed, but learning outcomes lag behind expectations.
2012+ Low-cost tablets and smartphones begin to dominate the market.

What Went Wrong ‚Äî The 7 Failures

1. Cost Overrun üí∞

Promised $100/laptop, delivered at ~$200. Strained government budgets and raised sustainability doubts.

2. Supplier Lock-in üîí

Signed 3-year exclusive deals for "corporate discounts". Couldn‚Äôt shift to cheaper components‚Äîclassic path dependence.

3. Infrastructure Gaps ‚ö°

Many communities lacked electricity and internet. Devices became "glorified toys" without full functionality.

4. Teacher Underestimation üë©‚Äçüè´

Assumed autonomous child-led learning, but teachers were essential and undertrained. Devices underused or misused.

5. Content Mismatch üìö

No localized curriculum-aligned content. Laptops seen as add-ons, not integrated into formal schooling.

6. Sustainability & Maintenance üîß

No repair capacity, no update roadmap, no end-of-life strategy. Broken devices piled up.

7. Competitive Blindspot üì±

Ignored rise of low-cost tablets and smartphones (e.g., Amazon Fire) that quickly undercut OLPC‚Äôs advantage.

Where It Worked

Uruguay: government integrated laptops into education, trained teachers, and ensured classroom use.

Some children gained first-ever technology access, sparking creativity and curiosity.

But successes were inconsistent and depended on local context and institutional support.

The Verdict

Outcome

By 2009, millions were distributed but educational transformation had not materialized at promised scale. ‚ÄúLofty goals clashed with the complex realities of the environments in which it operated.‚Äù

Cautionary Tale

OLPC is the cautionary tale of what happens when a social enterprise prioritizes rapid global scale over local context, embeddedness, and the fundamentals of readiness.

8. Key Lessons for Scaling SEs

Scale is not inherently good ‚Äî readiness matters more than speed.

Local context is non-negotiable ‚Äî what works in Uruguay won‚Äôt work everywhere.

Path dependence is real ‚Äî early decisions lock you in.

The 5Rs must be assessed honestly before scaling.

Korten‚Äôs progression holds: get effective, get efficient, then expand.

Technology is a tool, not a solution ‚Äî without ecosystem support, it fails.
