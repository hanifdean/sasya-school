1. Systems of Exchange (Biggart & Delbridge)

The Core Argument

Traditional economics treats "the market" as the default‚Äîany other form of exchange is seen as an imperfection or friction. Biggart & Delbridge argue this is wrong: there are qualitatively different systems of exchange, each with its own logic, social relations, and outcomes.

Key Insight

Markets are not natural or universal‚Äîthey are culturally constructed and socially embedded. The "free market" is just one type of exchange arena, not the ideal against which all others should be measured.

Three Approaches to Understanding Markets

1. Economic Approach (Traditional)

Markets as price-setting mechanisms. Assumes rational actors, complete information, homogeneous commodities. Deviations = "imperfections." Social relations = "friction."

Limitation: Treats markets as abstract, empirically empty concepts.

2. Markets as Social Structures

Markets are networks of concrete social relations. Position in a network affects outcomes. Key theorists: Granovetter (embeddedness), White, Burt, Baker.

Limitation: Focuses on structure of ties, not content or culture of ties.

3. Markets as Cultural Arenas

Markets as constructed social worlds with shared meanings, values, and beliefs. Culture shapes how actors define interests, evaluate options, and legitimate actions.

Key insight: "Categories of economic action are culturally variable and socially constructed."

The Classification Framework

Biggart & Delbridge combine network structure + cultural logic to classify different exchange systems:

Dimension What it captures
Logic of Action What motivates actors? Self-interest? Obligation? Solidarity? Status?
Structure of Relations How are actors connected? Anonymous? Network-embedded? Hierarchical?
Why This Matters for Social Innovation

If you're designing a social enterprise, you need to understand what kind of exchange system you're operating in (or creating). Imposing a "market" logic where solidarity or reciprocity governs will fail‚Äîand vice versa.

2. Community Economies in the Global South (Hossein & Kinyanjui)

The Core Argument

Western development institutions have historically dismissed informal, indigenous economies as "primitive" or "undeveloped." This reading argues the opposite: community economies are sophisticated, sustainable, and legitimate‚Äîand the Global South has been doing them for centuries.

The Problem with Western Development

Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs) and "modernization" projects imposed free-market orthodoxy on the Global South, ignoring existing community-based systems. The binary of capitalism vs. communism left no room for indigenous alternatives.

What Are Community Economies?

Economic activities rooted in self-reliance, solidarity, and collective action. They prioritize equity and social bonds over individual profit maximization.

ROSCAs (Rotating Savings & Credit Associations)

Groups where members contribute regularly and take turns receiving the pooled funds. Known by many names:

  • Indonesia: Arisan
  • Nigeria: Esusu
  • Kenya: Chama
  • Caribbean: Sou-sou, Partner
  • West Africa (Francophone): Tontines

ROSCAs are primarily organized by women and serve both financial and social functions.

Key Themes

üèõÔ∏è Colonial Legacy

Western epistemologies dominated discourse, labeling Global South practices as "inept" and "primitive." This narrative justified extraction and paternalistic development.

üå± Indigenous Knowledge

Philosophies like Ubuntu (Southern Africa), Ujamaa (Tanzania), and Kombit (Haiti) offer frameworks for cooperation that predate Western economics.

üë© Feminist Economics

Gibson-Graham's "diverse economies" framework recognizes that most economic activity happens outside formal capitalism‚Äîin households, communities, and informal networks, often led by women.

üèÜ Nobel Recognition

Elinor Ostrom won the Nobel Prize (2009) for showing that communities can sustainably manage common resources‚Äîcontrary to the "tragedy of the commons" narrative.

The Indonesia Case: Arisan

The reading highlights research by Ririn Haryani & Kelly Dombroski on arisan in Indonesia:

Arisan: More Than Savings
  • 100+ years of practice in Indonesia
  • Combines financial function (rotating credit) with social function (regular gatherings, care networks)
  • Reflects gotong royong (mutual assistance) values
  • Used by government for socialization programs (health, sanitation)
  • Members challenge "rational economic man" by operating collectively, not through self-interest
Arisan & Policy Influence

The authors note that arisan users were able to influence OJK (Indonesian Financial Services Authority) to issue regulations on financial inclusion. This represents grassroots influence on formal policy‚Äîcommunity practices shaping what "financial inclusion" means.

Critique of INGOs

The reading is critical of how international NGOs have co-opted indigenous practices:

The INGO Problem

Organizations like CARE receive millions to "teach" Village Savings & Loans (VSL) programs‚Äîessentially rebranding what African women have done for centuries. Western consultants profit from indigenous knowledge without crediting or compensating local communities.

3. Connecting the Two Readings

Both readings challenge the assumption that the "free market" is the natural or best form of economic organization:

Biggart & Delbridge

Academic/theoretical approach. Shows that exchange systems vary by logic of action and structure of relations. Markets are culturally constructed.

Hossein & Kinyanjui

Empirical/activist approach. Documents how community economies in the Global South embody alternative logics‚Äîsolidarity, reciprocity, collective care.

Implications for Social Innovation

1. Know Your Exchange System

Before designing a social enterprise, understand what exchange logic already exists. Imposing market logic on a solidarity-based community (or vice versa) will create friction.

2. Value Indigenous Knowledge

Don't reinvent the wheel. Community economies have solved problems for centuries. Partner with, learn from, and credit local knowledge rather than extracting it.

3. Question "Scaling" Assumptions

Western scaling models assume growth = good. Community economies prioritize sustainability, equity, and social cohesion over scale. What if staying small is the right strategy?

4. Recognize Multiple Payors

In community economies, "who pays" isn't just financial. Social obligations, reciprocity, and collective contribution are forms of payment. This connects to Week 3's "Who Pays?" theme.

5. Discussion Questions

For Class Discussion
  1. How would you classify arisan using Biggart & Delbridge's framework? What logic of action and structure of relations does it reflect?
  2. The readings critique Western development as paternalistic. But is there a role for external support in strengthening community economies? Where's the line?
  3. If community economies are so effective, why haven't they "scaled"? Is that even the right question to ask?
  4. How might social enterprises learn from rather than replace existing community economies?
  5. The arisan case shows grassroots influence on OJK policy. What conditions enabled this? How can community voices be amplified in other contexts?
